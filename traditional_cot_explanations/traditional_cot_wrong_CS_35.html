Meryll wants to write 35 multiple-choice questions and 15 problem-solving questions for her Chemistry class. She already has written 2/5 of the multiple-choice and 1/3 of the problem-solving questions. How many more questions does she need to write for both multiple-choice and problem-solving?

Step 2: Calculate how many problem-solving questions Meryll has written.

We need to multiply the total number of problem-solving questions by the fraction she has already written:

15 questions × 1/3 = 5 problem-solving questions

Step 3: Find the total number of questions Meryll needs to write.

We add the total number of multiple-choice and problem-solving questions:

35 questions + 15 questions = 50 questions

Step 4: Calculate the total number of questions Meryll has already written.

We add the number of multiple-choice and problem-solving questions she has already written:

14 questions + 5 questions = 19 questions
